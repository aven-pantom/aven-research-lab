# Rejected Path — Always-On Life Camera as the Product

## Why it initially seemed promising

If the goal is to capture more than photos, an always-on camera seems like the obvious complete answer. It would record everything, then let models extract memories later.

## What evidence weakened it

- **Verified:** Ego4D demonstrates the research value of large egocentric video, but it also required explicit collection design, consenting participants, and de-identification procedures.
- **Supported:** Lifelogging has existed for years without becoming a mainstream consumer behavior, partly because retrieval, privacy, social acceptability, and battery/storage burdens are hard.
- **Inferred:** More raw capture increases the burden of privacy and review faster than it increases user value.

## Which assumption failed

The failed assumption is that a new camera category needs more continuous recording. The stronger primitive is selective capture of meaningful transitions.

## Whether it may become viable later

Viable later if hardware, local models, consent signaling, social norms, battery, storage, and automatic redaction all improve substantially.

## Useful remnants

- Short buffered capture around explicit user-triggered events
- Local-only temporary memory that expires unless saved
- Redaction-first exports
- Queryable summaries without durable raw video

## Future signal that should trigger reconsideration

A consumer device with socially accepted visible capture state, strong on-device multimodal models, all-day battery, and trusted private memory controls.
