# Field Notes — 2026-07-20 — Insane Color Palettes

## Calling title

**The Camera That Finds the Colors You Were Too Polite to Use**

## Initial signal

Max shared an Instagram reel titled/captioned `UGLY MAXXING (COLOR EDITION)` and described an app idea:

```text
take picture → shows insane color pallets
```

## What caught attention

The phrase “insane color palettes” is stronger than “AI palette generator.”

A normal palette app tries to make color safe. This idea is about making color daring, weird, memorable, and usable.

## Visible reel evidence

**Verified:** The public Instagram page shows creator `@anjeees`, caption `UGLY MAXXING (COLOR EDITION)`, hashtags including `#uglymaxxing` and `#uglycolorcombinations`, and a selfie/design-student visual style with high-contrast black/gray/white composition.

**Verified:** The browser exposed a 9.23s 720×1280 video element, but only as a blob URL. Full frame analysis was not available.

## Product intuition

The app should not just extract dominant colors from a picture. That already exists.

The app should act like a **taste amplifier**:

1. capture a photo;
2. infer its atmosphere/material/light/style;
3. extract boring-safe palettes;
4. mutate them into several high-taste extremes;
5. name each palette like a fashion/design mood;
6. show where each color should be used;
7. generate a shareable card.

## The important word

`Insane` does not mean random. It means:

- unexpected but coherent;
- ugly-beautiful;
- high contrast;
- subcultural;
- editorial;
- almost wrong;
- memorable;
- usable by someone with taste.

## Candidate palette modes

- Safe Pull — faithful colors from the photo.
- Editorial Clash — high-tension but usable.
- Ugly Maxx — intentionally awkward, acidic, strange.
- Luxury Rot — muted, decayed, expensive.
- Plastic Night — synthetic/neon/dark.
- Film Bruise — cinematic, dirty, desaturated.
- Market Heat — bright, informal, street/fabric/food energy.
- Gallery Cold — austere, pale, negative-space palette.

## Open questions

- Can the app identify atmosphere better than simple dominant-color extraction?
- Should output optimize for fashion, UI, interiors, posters, brand identity, or social cards?
- Should the user choose a vibe mode before capture or after?
- Can palette quality be judged by contrast/accessibility plus taste heuristics?
- Can it become a daily camera habit rather than a one-off generator?

## Product risk

If it only outputs hex swatches, it becomes a commodity.

If it outputs named palette worlds with role mapping and shareable artifacts, it becomes a creative tool.
