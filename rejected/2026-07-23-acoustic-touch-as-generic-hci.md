# Rejected Path — Acoustic Touch as Generic HCI Surface

## Why it initially seemed promising

The 2009 Lamb-wave multi-touch plate and newer flexible acoustic waveguide work both suggest acoustic touch surfaces could become large, cheap, low-wiring interfaces.

## What weakened it

As a standalone HCI surface, the idea becomes too familiar: touch tables, smart surfaces, acoustic localization, and low-cost input panels. That path does not expose a sufficiently strange capability by itself.

## Assumption that failed

The initial assumption was that the strongest Vibe was `large surfaces can become touchscreens without dense electronics`.

The stronger assumption is different: **embodied machines can feel through their load-bearing structure**.

## Useful remnants

- HCI touch surfaces remain a good benchtop experiment environment.
- The 2009 Lamb-wave plate provides evidence that the primitive is old and newly worth revisiting.
- A desktop acoustic plate could serve as a cheap calibration/drift testbed before robot hardware.

## Future signal that should trigger reconsideration

Reopen this path if a new paper or open-source project demonstrates acoustic touch surfaces that can robustly infer multi-contact force/material/shear under changing mounts and everyday noise, or if a manufacturer ships a practical passive acoustic touch material.
